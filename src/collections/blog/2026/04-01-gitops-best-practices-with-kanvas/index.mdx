## GitOps Is Right — But Something Is Still Missing

The principles behind GitOps are sound. Declarative infrastructure, version-controlled state, automated reconciliation via tools like Flux and Argo CD — these patterns have materially improved how teams manage Kubernetes at scale. Yet platform teams consistently report the same friction points:

- **Cognitive overload**: YAML sprawl across hundreds of files in multiple repositories makes it nearly impossible to reason about the full state of your platform
- **Onboarding drag**: New engineers can spend weeks just mapping out what a cluster actually contains before they can contribute safely
- **Drift blind spots**: Reconcilers catch drift, but they don't show you *why* drift occurred or help you understand the blast radius of a proposed change
- **Review bottlenecks**: Pull requests for infrastructure changes are notoriously hard to review — raw YAML diffs don't communicate intent

<Link to="/cloud-native-management/kanvas">Kanvas</Link> doesn't replace your GitOps toolchain. It wraps it in a visual interface that makes the intended state of your infrastructure legible to everyone on the team — from the SRE debugging a production incident at 2 AM to the platform engineer designing the next environment topology.

## Best Practice 1: Treat Your Canvas as a Single Source of Visual Truth

The first GitOps principle is that Git is the source of truth. Kanvas extends this with a complementary principle: the canvas is the source of *visual* truth. Every element you place on a Kanvas canvas corresponds directly to a Kubernetes resource or cloud-native component — not a diagram of one, but the actual manifest.

When you import your existing Helm charts, Kustomize overlays, or raw manifests into Kanvas, the platform renders them as interactive components. Edit a component's configuration in the canvas, and the underlying YAML updates accordingly. Push that to Git, and your GitOps controller reconciles the change to the cluster.

This bi-directional relationship means your diagrams can never drift from your declared state. The canvas *is* the manifest.

<Callout type="tip" title="Start with an import">
  <p>If you're adopting Kanvas on an existing platform, begin by importing your production manifests. This gives your team an instant visual audit of what you're actually running — often surfacing forgotten resources and undocumented dependencies that YAML files alone obscure.</p>
</Callout>

## Best Practice 2: Design Environments, Not Just Applications

Most teams using GitOps manage their environment definitions (dev, staging, production) as separate overlays or values files. The relationships between environments — what's promoted, what's pinned, what's tested before a change reaches production — are usually implicit, living in CI pipeline scripts or tribal knowledge.

Kanvas lets you model these relationships explicitly on the canvas. You can lay out your full environment topology, annotate promotion gates, and make the intended flow of changes visible to everyone with access to the workspace.

This is particularly powerful when combined with Kanvas's Operator mode, which connects directly to your live clusters. You can flip between your declared state (Designer mode) and the actual running state (Operator mode) to verify that your GitOps controller has converged — and to investigate when it hasn't.

## Best Practice 3: Use the Catalog to Standardize Deployment Patterns

One of the biggest challenges for platform teams is enforcing consistent deployment patterns across application teams without becoming a bottleneck. GitOps helps here — shared Helm charts and base Kustomize configurations provide reusable building blocks — but discovering and correctly using those patterns is still a manual, documentation-dependent process.

The <Link to="/cloud-native-management/catalog">Kanvas Catalog</Link> provides a visual, searchable library of reusable infrastructure patterns. Platform teams can publish approved deployment patterns — complete with resource configurations, network policies, and observability instrumentation — that application teams can clone directly onto their canvas and deploy.

Because every catalog item is a live, deployable design rather than a static diagram, teams aren't just looking at a reference architecture. They're starting from a configuration that has already been validated and approved.

## Best Practice 4: Make Infrastructure Reviews Visual

Infrastructure pull requests are notoriously hard to review. A diff showing 47 changed lines across three YAML files communicates almost nothing about whether the intended change is correct or safe.

Kanvas addresses this directly. When you connect your workspace to a Git repository, Kanvas can render the *diff* between two infrastructure states visually — showing you not just what changed, but how the topology and relationships of your system shift as a result of the change. Reviewers can see at a glance whether a new Deployment is correctly connected to a Service and Ingress, whether resource limits are set, and whether the change touches shared infrastructure.

<Callout type="note">
  <p>Visual infrastructure reviews dramatically reduce the cognitive load on reviewers and catch category errors — like accidentally removing a network policy or orphaning a persistent volume — that raw YAML diffs miss entirely.</p>
</Callout>

## Best Practice 5: Connect Real-Time State to Your GitOps Loop

GitOps reconcilers operate on a loop: they compare desired state (Git) to observed state (cluster) and correct any divergence. But the reconciler's view of "observed state" is Kubernetes API objects — not the human-readable view of what the cluster is actually doing.

Kanvas's Operator mode bridges this gap. Connect your clusters to your Kanvas workspace, and you get a live visual representation of your running infrastructure — pod status, service connectivity, resource consumption — overlaid on the same canvas where you design and declare your desired state. When a reconciliation error occurs, you can see it visually, in context, without context-switching to multiple CLIs and dashboards.

For on-call engineers, this is particularly valuable. Instead of mentally assembling a picture of your infrastructure from scattered terminal output, you can open Kanvas and immediately see the current state of the affected system.

## GitOps at Scale: Where Kanvas Fits

Platform engineering teams operating at scale manage dozens of clusters, hundreds of application teams, and millions of lines of infrastructure configuration. No single tool solves all of this — and Kanvas doesn't try to.

What Kanvas provides is the human interface layer that GitOps has always been missing. The declarative power of Git-backed infrastructure is real, but it was designed for machines to consume. Kanvas makes that same infrastructure legible, navigable, and actionable for the engineers who build and operate it.

The <Link to="/cloud-native-management/meshery">Meshery</Link> platform underlying Kanvas supports management across multiple clusters and cloud providers, meaning the visual layer scales with your infrastructure rather than becoming its own bottleneck.

## Getting Started

If your team is already practicing GitOps, onboarding Kanvas is straightforward:

1. **Import your existing manifests** — Kanvas renders your declared state immediately
2. **Connect your clusters** — Operator mode shows you real-time observed state alongside declared state
3. **Publish your patterns** — Add your approved deployment templates to the Catalog for application teams to reuse
4. **Review changes visually** — Integrate Kanvas into your infrastructure PR workflow to make reviews faster and more reliable

GitOps gave platform engineering teams control over infrastructure delivery. Kanvas gives them visibility into what they're controlling.
